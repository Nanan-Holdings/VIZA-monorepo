# What Trump's Order Making English the Official Language Means for Immigrants

Learn how Trump’s executive order making English the U.S. official language could affect immigrants, and explore resources to help you overcome language barriers.

![Image 6](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/68a3050860dd863f875cffa9/68c37a143cfa1f6a67b38d41_50057959533_e89f651dba_c.jpeg)

President Donald Trump has signed an executive order declaring English the official language of the United States. This marks the first time in U.S. history that a single language has been given official status at the federal level. While the order does not prohibit government agencies from offering services in other languages, it rescinds a policy established in 2000 by President Bill Clinton that required federal agencies and recipients of federal funding to provide language assistance to non-English speakers.

## How the Order Affects Immigrants

For immigrants who rely on government documents, health care forms, and voting materials in languages other than English, the order introduces uncertainty. While agencies and federally funded organizations can still choose to provide multilingual services, they are no longer required to do so. This could mean fewer translated materials for those who need them to navigate essential services.

Immigrant advocacy groups have criticized the order, warning that it could make it harder for non-English speakers to access critical information and participate fully in civic life. The Congressional Asian Pacific American Caucus [called the move](https://capac.house.gov/press-release/joint-statement-tri-caucus-chairs-president-trumps-executive-order-declaring-english) a “thinly veiled attempt to allow federal agencies to discriminate against immigrants,” adding that “people who speak another language other than English are just as American as those who do. That’s been true since the founding of our country.”

## The Politics of Language in America

The debate over English as the official language is not new. More than 30 states have already adopted English as their official language, and past efforts to do so at the federal level have [repeatedly failed](https://www.cnn.com/2018/05/20/us/english-us-official-language-trnd/). Supporters argue that recognizing English formally reflects the reality of American life, where nearly 80% of the population speaks only English. They also see it as a unifying measure, reinforcing a shared national identity.

However, critics argue that the U.S. has always been linguistically diverse. The country’s founding documents may have been written in English, but translations were quickly made into Dutch and German to allow for broader understanding. Throughout history, state governments have published laws in multiple languages, and bilingual education has played a role in integrating immigrant communities.

## What Comes Next?

In practical terms, the order may have a limited immediate impact, since it does not require agencies to eliminate existing language services. However, it opens the door for agencies and federally funded organizations to reduce or remove translation assistance at their discretion. This could affect millions of people— [according](https://www.census.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2023/language-at-home-acs-5-year.html) to the U.S. Census Bureau, nearly 68 million residents speak a language other than English at home.

While learning English has long been a path to economic opportunity and civic engagement, language access policies have historically helped bridge gaps for those still acquiring English skills. The full effects of the order will depend on how government agencies choose to respond and whether future administrations uphold or reverse the policy.

## Tips for Immigrants

Trump’s executive order may lead to changes in how services are provided, but there are still steps immigrants can take to navigate this shift, including:

1.   **Focus on English Language Learning**

While the order does not eliminate language services altogether, becoming proficient in English remains one of the best ways to engage fully in American society. Many community programs, libraries, and nonprofit organizations, such as the [International Rescue Committee](https://www.rescue.org/announcement/english-class-registration) (IRC) and [Catholic Charities](https://www.ccasfnm.org/esl-656663.html), offer free or low-cost language classes. [This government site](https://www.usa.gov/learn-english) has some helpful resources on ways to learn English.
2.   **Stay Informed**

Keep track of any changes to government services or language access policies. While some agencies may continue providing multilingual materials, it’s important to verify what services are available in your language, particularly for healthcare, voting, and legal matters.
3.   **Use Online Translation Tools**

In the absence of translated materials, online tools like Google Translate can help you understand key documents or instructions. However, always double-check important information (like legal documents) with a professional translator if possible.
4.   **Reach Out to Community Organizations**

Many immigrant advocacy groups and local nonprofits, such as the [Immigrant Legal Resource Center](https://www.ilrc.org/) and the [National Immigration Law Center](https://www.nilc.org/), offer assistance in multiple languages. These organizations can be valuable resources for help with forms, navigating government systems, and understanding your rights.
5.   **Advocate for Language Access**

If you face challenges accessing important services due to language barriers, consider joining or supporting advocacy groups, like the [ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/), pushing for the preservation of language access programs. These groups often work to ensure that all residents can access essential services regardless of what language they speak.
