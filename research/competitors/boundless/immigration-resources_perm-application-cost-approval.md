## What Is a PERM Labor Certification?

The permanent labor certification program (or PERM) is run by the U.S. Department of Labor’s (DOL’s) Employment and Training Administration (ETA). Its primary purpose is to assess whether the hiring of a non-U.S. citizen employee would prevent equally capable U.S. workers from being hired for similar positions. ETA evaluates whether recruiting a non-citizen would have detrimental effects on the working conditions or wages of U.S. workers employed in a similar field.

Employers looking to hire a non-U.S. citizen must first obtain a PERM certification _before_ they can submit a petition to U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) for an employment-based visa (such as an EB-2). Once an employer obtains a certification, they can proceed to filing USCIS [Form I-140](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-140-explained) (officially called the, “Immigrant Petition for Alien Workers”). When reviewing the petition, USCIS must determine:

*   Whether the job in question is equivalent to the job assessed by ETA
*   Whether the foreign worker is qualified for the job

But before the employer can get as far as Form I-140, they must first make their case with the U.S. Department of Labor. In the following guide, we’ll discuss the process for obtaining a PERM certification.

## PERM Application Process

To obtain a permanent labor certification, employers must file [Form 9089](https://www.dol.gov/sites/dolgov/files/ETA/oflc/pdfs/9089form.pdf) (officially called the “Application for Permanent Employment Certification”). The PERM application may be filed in several different ways, depending on the circumstances. In this section, we will focus on the “basic labor certification process,” as outlined in [§ 656.17 of the Code of Federal Regulations](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/20/656.17) (CFR) and in the [instructions](https://www.dol.gov/sites/dolgov/files/ETA/oflc/pdfs/9089inst.pdf) for Form 9089.

### Basic Requirements

According to Federal Regulations ([CFR § 656.10 (c)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/20/656.10)), in order to apply for a permanent labor certification, an employer must attest to the following:

*   That the job listing was made available to U.S. workers
*   That the wages offered to the prospective employee are greater than or equal to the “prevailing wages” for the given field. Here, “prevailing wages” refers to either the wages set forth by a relevant collective bargaining agreement (CBA) or the average wages for employees in a similar field (see [CFR § 656.40](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/20/656.40)). To meet this condition, the employer _must_ request a Prevailing Wage Determination (PWD) from the State Workforce Agency (SWA) for the relevant state. A list of SWA offices can be found [on the DOL website](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/eta/wotc/contact/state-workforce-agencies).
*   That the employer is able to put the prospective employee on payroll either before or on the day of their arrival in the United States
*   That any U.S.-citizen job applicants have been denied for lawful reasons
*   That the wages are not dependent on bonuses, commissions, or financial incentives of any kind. Unless the employer can ensure the prospective employee will continue to earn a wage greater than or equal to the “prevailing wages” mentioned above
*   That the employer has enough money to actually pay the promised wages
*   That, in making this job offer, the employer did not discriminate based on age, sex, religion, race, creed, color, nationality, disability, or citizenship
*   That the job in question did not become available because of a strike or labor stoppage
*   That the working conditions and terms of employment meet any relevant legal standards, including all Federal, state, and local statutes
*   That the job in question is a full-time, permanent position
*   That the employer is not the same person as the non-U.S.-citizen

### Pre-Filing Recruitment

In addition to meeting these basic requirements, the employer will have to conduct certain “recruitment steps” prior to filing their application (as indicated in [CFR § 656.17](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/20/656.17)). If the job opening is for a “professional occupation” — in which case the job applicant must have at least a bachelor’s degree, or equivalent experience, to be hired — the employer must, at a minimum, place a job order with SWA for at least 30 days, print at least 2 advertisements in 2 separate Sunday editions of a popular newspaper, and select 3 additional recruitment methods from the following list:

*   Advertisements on Television or Radio
*   Local and ethnic newspapers
*   Job listings on the business’ website
*   Job fairs
*   University campus recruitment
*   Trade organizations
*   Recruitment agencies
*   Job search sites, like Indeed or Monster
*   Incentivized employee referrals
*   College campus placement programs

For “nonprofessional occupations,” the employer must place a job order with SWA and print 2 newspaper advertisements. All the recruitment activities _must_ take place during the 6 months leading up to the filing date — but no less than 30 days prior.

### Giving Notice

The employer will need to give advance notice that they will be submitting the PERM application. This notice should be documented and given either to the union representative of any current employees filling similar roles or, if no such representative exists, to the workers themselves at their place of employment. The notice should meet the following conditions:

*   It must be given anytime between 30 and 180 days prior to submitting the PERM application.
*   It must contain the wage offered to the prospective employee — and that wage must exceed the prevailing wages of the given field, as determined by SWA.
*   It must contain the address of the Certifying Officer handling the application.
*   It must state that anyone may send proof pertaining to the PERM application directly to the Certifying Officer.
*   It must state that a PERM application will soon be filed.

Once the above-mentioned requirements are met, and notice is given within the appropriate time frame, the employer may start filling out the permanent labor certification application.

### The Application

To obtain a PERM certification, employers will need to file ETA Form 9089. They can submit this form either [online](https://www.plc.doleta.gov/splash.cfm) or through the mail.

If using snail mail, the employer, the foreign worker, and the person preparing the application should sign the form before sending it to [the DOL processing center](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/eta/foreign-labor/contact). If submitting online, they will need to sign the certification as soon as they receive it. For auditing purposes, all paperwork gathered during the application process should be kept for a minimum of 5 years following the submission of Form 9089.

In order to successfully obtain PERM approval, and to avoid any unnecessary audits, the employer must be sure to fully complete the application in an honest and consistent fashion. It may be helpful — for whoever is completing the form — to gather some basic information ahead of time, including:

*   The full legal name and address of the business’ main office or headquarters
*   The total number of employees
*   The year the business began
*   The employer’s 9-digit Employer Identification Number (EIN)
*   The North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code. This can be found on [the NAICS website](https://www.naics.com/search/).
*   Contact information for the business’ main point of contact, including email and phone number. This must be different than the attorney’s contact information, which may also be provided elsewhere on the application

As mentioned above, in the “Requirements” section, the employer will need to request a PWD from the appropriate SWA office — and **_they should do this at least 150 days prior to filing_**. The person completing the PERM application will use the PWD to provide information such as the following:

*   The prevailing wage tracking number (if given)
*   The Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) code. The code should refer to the occupation entered on the initial PWD
*   The prevailing wage rate given by SWA
*   The source of the prevailing wage. Sources include:
    *   Occupational Employment Statistics (OES)
    *   Collective Bargaining Agreement (OBA)
    *   McNamara-O’Hara Service Contract Act (SCA)

The employer should be sure to document the recruitment process and retain information regarding the job itself — including the duties and hiring requirements. The person completing the form will need to answer questions related to the following:

*   The recruitment methods used by the employer
*   The start and end dates of the SWA job order
*   The names of the newspapers containing the job advertisements
*   The dates of all recruitment events (if applicable)
*   The dates of any job listings on sites like Indeed and Monster (if applicable)
*   Whether the employee will need to speak more than 1 language to do their job
*   Whether the employee needs more than a high school diploma

The employer will have to provide details regarding the prospective foreign employee, including their address, phone number, and information regarding their work experience. And if someone other than the employer is preparing the application, they must provide some basic information about themselves, including their name, job title, and email address.

Once the Form 9089 is complete, and all the above requirements have been satisfied, the employer may submit their application.

## PERM Processing Time

The average PERM processing time is **483 days**. Keep in mind that PERM processing times can vary significantly depending on your situation. For a more thorough breakdown, visit [the DOL website](https://flag.dol.gov/processingtimes?_ga=2.183409888.755611771.1640123997-1173166104.1640123997).

To speed up the process, an employer can submit the PERM application electronically using the [Permanent Online System](https://www.plc.doleta.gov/eta_start.cfm?actiontype=home). Using the PERM tracker, they can keep tabs on the progress of their case. There are benefits to filing the petition electronically, including:

*   Faster PERM processing time
*   Being able to track the PERM application
*   Retrieving copies of previously submitted 9089s
*   Quick confirmation of PERM status
*   Being able to quickly reuse information on multiple applications

This is especially useful if the employer plans on filing more than one Form 9089.

### What Is the PERM 365-Day Rule?

The 365-day rule for PERM applications allows [H-1B visa](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-h-1b-visa-explained) holders to extend their status in one-year increments while their PERM application is still being processed by the Department of Labor. This helps foreign workers avoid employment gaps while waiting, since PERM processing can take 18 months or more.

**To qualify for the 1-year extension:**

*   The PERM application must have been pending for at least 365 days.
*   The H-1B worker must be in valid status at the time of the request.
*   The extension request must be submitted before the current H-1B status expires.

This rule provides a way for workers to continue employment without disruption during the lengthy green card process.

## PERM Cost

While there’s no filing fee for Form 9089, there are a number of PERM-related costs. Employers may, for instance, want to hire an attorney who can prepare all the paperwork and provide counsel throughout the process. Lawyer fees may vary, but employers can expect to pay, at a minimum, between $2000 and $6000.

The employer will also need to cover all expenses related to recruitment. This means they’ll have to pay for 2 separate print advertisements, which [can cost around $500 per ad](https://fitsmallbusiness.com/newspaper-advertising-costs/). This price can vary depending on a number of factors, including the circulation of the newspaper, the size and color of the advertisement, and the location of the news outlet.

In addition to these PERM-related costs, the employer will have to cover all filing fees for any forms filed with USCIS. The fee for Form I-140 — which is to be filed by the employer on behalf of a [green card](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-green-card-explained) beneficiary — is $715.

These costs don’t include any of the additional overhead, or other incidental fees, associated with the hiring process.

## Following PERM Approval: EB-2

Once the employer receives the certification, they can then begin filing USCIS Form I-140, effectively commencing [the green card process](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-green-card-explained) for the foreign employee.

The petitioner must submit I-140, together with the permanent labor certification (if required), within 180 days following the date of PERM approval. Otherwise, the certification will expire, and they will need to go through the DOL filing process again. Unless otherwise stated by USCIS, a [Form I-140](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-140-explained) without the PERM application — and all the necessary signatures and stamps — will be denied.

When reviewing the petitioner’s I-140, [USCIS will evaluate](https://www.uscis.gov/policy-manual/volume-6-part-e-chapter-6) whether the foreign worker was sufficiently qualified for the position when the PERM application was first submitted. Qualifications will vary depending on the type of visa sought by the green card applicant. One common type is the [EB-2](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/eb2-visa-explained) visa.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

### PERM FAQs

What are the potential risks for an employer if the PERM labor certification is denied, and how can these risks be mitigated?

If a PERM application is denied, employers risk losing their investment in recruitment efforts and may need to restart the process, delaying the green card process for the employee.

To reduce these risks, keep detailed records of recruitment efforts and work with an immigration attorney to avoid mistakes, and maintain detailed records to meet Department of Labor (DOL) audit requirements.

What happens if a U.S. worker applies for the position during the PERM recruitment process, and how does that affect the application?

If a U.S. worker applies and meets the minimum qualifications for the job, the employer may not proceed with the PERM process for that specific role. Employers must evaluate each U.S. applicant carefully and in good faith.

Rejecting an applicant must be based on legitimate, job-related reasons and not to favor the foreign worker. Failure to properly evaluate U.S. applicants can result in application denial or DOL audits.

How does the recruitment process under PERM differ from standard recruitment, and what are the specific requirements to ensure compliance with DOL regulations?

The PERM recruitment process is more structured and regulated than standard hiring practices. Employers must place ads in prescribed locations, run specific types of media campaigns (newspapers, job boards), and adhere to strict timelines.

Each step must be documented meticulously to demonstrate that no qualified U.S. worker was available for the position. Noncompliance can lead to denial or audits.

How do prevailing wage determinations impact the success of a PERM application, and what should employers consider when requesting a wage determination?

The DOL’s prevailing wage determination is a key factor in PERM applications. The wage must meet or exceed the prevailing wage for the position and location where the job will be performed.

Employers should carefully define the job duties and requirements to ensure that the wage determination aligns with their budget and expectations. Inaccuracies or disputes over the wage level can lead to delays or application denial.

What should employers do if there are changes to the job position or business structure during the PERM process, and how can these changes affect the outcome?

Any significant changes to the job role (such as a change in job location, duties, or requirements) or company structure (mergers, acquisitions) can impact the PERM process.

In some cases, a new application may need to be filed, restarting the process. Employers should consult with legal counsel if changes occur to ensure compliance with DOL requirements and avoid jeopardizing the application.

How does the possibility of a DOL audit impact the preparation and filing of a PERM application, and what triggers an audit?

While not all PERM applications are audited, certain factors may increase the likelihood, such as unusually high job requirements or wage levels that do not align with the job market.

Employers should prepare every application as if it could be audited, ensuring all recruitment and job details are documented. An audit can delay the process significantly, so being prepared helps reduce any risks.

What are the consequences of discrepancies between the ETA Form 9089 (PERM application) and Form I-140, and how can employers ensure consistency between the two?

Discrepancies between the information provided in the PERM application (ETA Form 9089) and the immigrant petition (Form I-140) can lead to denials or requests for evidence (RFEs).

Be sure to maintain consistency in job titles, duties, requirements, and wage details across both forms. Employers should work closely with an immigration attorney to review both filings to avoid any conflicts that could delay the green card process.

How can an employer address the challenge of meeting PERM advertising requirements in a highly specialized or niche job market?

In niche fields where qualified candidates are rare, complying with PERM recruitment requirements can be difficult. Employers must still follow the same recruitment steps (newspaper ads, job boards, etc.), even if it seems unnecessary for specialized roles.

Using industry-specific job boards and professional networks in the recruitment process can help meet compliance requirements and improve results.
