When applying for a [spousal visa](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/cr1-ir1-spouse-visa), the appropriate process to follow will depend on where the spouse seeking a visa card currently lives.

If they live and apply from within the United States, they’ll go through a process known as “[adjustment of status](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/adjustment-status-process-explained).” The [family sponsorship form](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-130-explained) (I-130) and [adjustment of status application](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-485-explained) (I-485) — are usually filed separately. When these two forms are filed together it is referred to as “concurrent filing”.

When the spouse seeking a green card lives and applies from _abroad_, however, the procedure to follow is known as **“**[‍](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/green-card-consular-processing)**,”** because it involves officers at U.S. consulates and embassies. Applications filed from abroad are handled by the National Visa Center ([NVC](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/what-is-the-national-visa-center)), which is part of the U.S. Department of State.

Each process has its own [timeline](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-long-does-marriage-green-card-take), as well as a different set of application forms, [supporting documents](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/what-documents-for-marriage-green-card), and [costs](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/green-card-cost), but the overall eligibility requirements are the same. While most couples have no choice between the two options, some couples may be in a position to decide for themselves whether one approach is better than the other for their specific circumstances and goals.

## When You Don’t Have a Choice

Most couples don’t actually have a choice in whether to apply for a [marriage green card](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/marriage-based-green-cards-explained) from within the United States or from abroad. Let’s explore each scenario where a couple must follow one particular process:

### Adjustment of Status

**If the sponsoring spouse is a green card holder (not a U.S. citizen), and the spouse seeking a green card lives and plans to stay in the United States while their green card application is pending**, they would need to apply for adjustment of status.

In this case, however, the spouse seeking a green card must be able to maintain a valid immigration status in the United States while they wait. Maintaining a valid immigration status means having a valid visa — such as an [H-1B work visa](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-h-1b-visa-explained) or an [F-1 student visa](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/f-1-student-visa-explained) — until they’ve filed their [green card application](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-green-card-explained/#the-green-card-application) (Using [Form I-485](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-485-explained)). That’s because they’ll need to wait until a [visa number](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-to-read-the-visa-bulletin/#important-termsnbspin-the-visa-bulletin) becomes available before applying for the green card from within the United States. (Visa numbers are immediately available to spouses of U.S. citizens but not to spouses of green card holders.) In practice, this means that the spouse seeking a green card must have a valid visa for up to 2.5 years after filing the marriage-based green card application. (Learn more from our detailed guide to the [marriage-based green card timeline](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-long-does-marriage-green-card-take).)

### Consular Processing

**If the sponsoring spouse is a green card holder (not a U.S. citizen), and the spouse seeking a green card lives in the United States but cannot maintain a valid immigration status**, they’ll need to apply from abroad and follow the consular process. Not being able to maintain a valid immigration status means their current visa has expired or is due to expire, and they’re unable to secure an extension or an entirely new visa before they file their [green card application](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-green-card-explained) (Using [Form I-485](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-485-explained)).

If they stay in the United States for [six months or longer](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/green-cards-and-prior-immigration-violations/#overstays-unlawful-presence) past their temporary visa’s expiration date, they would be barred from re-entering the United States for three or 10 years once they leave and attempt to return (see [this article](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/green-cards-and-prior-immigration-violations) for important details about the consequences of “overstaying”).

They would also need to obtain a document called a “[waiver of inadmissibility](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-to-apply-waiver-of-inadmissibility)” (basically, “forgiveness” from the U.S. government) _before_ they leave the United States, which would allow them to avoid the three or 10-year bars to re-entry, so it’s essential to leave the United States within six months of the temporary visa’s expiration and continue the green card process from abroad.

**If the spouse seeking a green card lives abroad and does _not_ have a valid visa to enter the United States**, then they generally must apply from abroad and follow the consular process.

The drawback here is that the couple may need to live apart while waiting for green card approval, though not necessarily the entire time. Although a spouse seeking a green card may technically [visit their spouse in the United States](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/when-can-i-visit-my-spouse-in-the-united-states) with a tourist visa, this option is generally discouraged because of the risks involved. More importantly, if they [visit the United States as a tourist](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/b-1-b-2-visitor-visa-explained) and then apply for a green card _while_ in the United States, they would potentially be violating U.S. immigration law. It’s usually much easier for the sponsoring spouse to visit their spouse abroad. Learn more about visiting your spouse with a pending spousal visa application [here](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/when-can-i-visit-my-spouse-in-the-united-states).

## When You Have a Choice

Some couples may choose where and how to apply for a marriage-based green card. Let’s discuss when one process may be a more suitable approach:

### Living in the United States with a valid visa

If the sponsoring spouse is a U.S. citizen, and the spouse seeking a green card lives in the United States and currently has valid immigration status (that is, they have a temporary visa, such as an [H-1B work visa](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-h-1b-visa-explained) or an [F-1 student visa](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/f-1-student-visa-explained), that has _not_ expired), they can choose between adjustment of status and consular processing.

The best option depends on the couple’s goals for the next year or two:

### Option 1: Apply from abroad through consular processing

This is generally the best option if, before settling in the United States, the spouse seeking a green card wants to spend a year in their home country for any reason (for example, to finish their education or sell their home).

### Option 2: Apply from within the United States through an adjustment of status

This is generally the best option if the spouse seeking a green card wants to stay in the United States while their green card application is pending. The spouse seeking a green card will be able to stay in the United States even if their current visa (or other immigration status) has expired. Any amount of time that the spouse seeking a green card “[overstayed](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/green-cards-and-prior-immigration-violations/#overstays-unlawful-presence)” would generally be waived as a benefit of marriage to a U.S. citizen. It’s important to understand, however, that it is always at the discretion of USCIS how to interpret visa overstays during the green card application process. Learn how to [check your green card status](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-to-check-green-card-status).

The spouse seeking a green card may also follow this process if they live in the United States but are on a short international trip. They should keep in mind, however, the consequences of marrying a U.S. citizen or green card holder within 90 days of entering the United States on a temporary visa, a guideline known as the [“90 Day Rule.”](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/90-day-rule-explained)

### Living together abroad

If both spouses live together abroad, there are a couple of ways to apply for a marriage-based green card:

### Option 1: Apply as if the sponsoring spouse were living in the United States

If the sponsoring spouse is a _U.S. citizen_, you can follow the [process for most spouses living abroad and married to a U.S. citizen](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/guides/us-family-spouse-citizen-abroad), and you will need to take additional steps (see [below](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/adjustment-of-status-vs-consular-processing/#additional-steps)).

If the sponsoring spouse is a _green card holde_ r, you would follow the [process for most spouses living abroad and married to a green card holder](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/guides/us-family-spouse-noncitizen-abroad), and you will need to take additional steps (see [below](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/adjustment-of-status-vs-consular-processing/#additional-steps)).

Some couples may send their application materials to an international USCIS office rather than to a U.S.-based USCIS office. This option is available in countries or regions where USCIS has an office (check this list of [office locations](https://www.uscis.gov/about-us/find-uscis-office/international-immigration-offices)). The biggest advantage of filing with an international USCIS office is faster processing time compared with filing with USCIS in the United States. Depending on which office you file with, it could shave months off of the application process.

### Option 2: Apply based on “exceptional circumstances”

For couples with “exceptional circumstances,” they may submit their application materials directly to their nearest U.S. consulate or embassy. This special process is called “[Direct Consular Filing](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/direct-consular-filing)” and is the fastest way to get a marriage-based green card: usually about three months from start to finish. Not all U.S. consulates offer this filing option, however, and the State Department does not provide an official list of those consulates.

Examples of exceptional circumstances that qualify for this type of processing include the following (see the [full list on the State Department’s website](https://www.uscis.gov/archive/archive-news/uscis-centralizes-filing-form-i-130-0)):

*   If the sponsoring spouse is in the U.S. military, is stationed abroad, and is deployed or must transfer under short notice
*   If either spouse needs urgent medical treatment in the United States
*   If either spouse faces an imminent threat to their personal safety

### Additional steps

Regardless of which option you choose to follow above, if both spouses live abroad, then the sponsoring spouse will also need to prove one of the following:

1.   That they’ve [maintained residency](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/prove-domicile-form-i-864) in the United States (for example, by maintaining a home or employment in the United States and filing a U.S. income-tax return).
2.   That they intend to re-establish residency in the United States once the marriage-based green card application is approved. To do so, the sponsoring spouse will need to submit both evidence and an affidavit (sworn statement) stating that they intend to return to the United States. Evidence can include signing a lease on a U.S. home, registering your children in a U.S. school, opening a U.S. bank account, and taking a U.S.-based job.

## Quick Reference Guide

Not sure which option is best for you, as the spouse seeking a green card? Here’s a quick summary of when it’s likely better to apply from within the United States (adjustment of status) and from abroad ([consular processing](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/green-card-consular-processing)):

### Consider using adjustment of status if:

*   You physically live in the United States and want to spend the next year living here.
*   Your sponsoring spouse is a green card holder, and you’ll be able to maintain a valid immigration status in the United States for the next 2.5 years.

### Consider using consular processing if:

*   You want to spend the next year living abroad, and your sponsoring spouse is a U.S. citizen.
*   Your sponsoring spouse is a green card holder, and you won’t be able to maintain a valid immigration status in the United States for at least 2.5 years after filing the [family sponsorship form](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-130-explained) (Form I-130).

## Special Considerations

### Traveling internationally

Regardless of which process you follow, there will be some limitations on when and where you can travel as the spouse seeking a green card:

*   If you choose to go through _consular processing_, it can be [difficult to travel to the United States](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/when-can-i-visit-my-spouse-in-the-united-states) while your application is pending — even just for a short trip. This is the case even if you already have a valid tourist visa.
*   If you choose to go through _adjustment of status_, you may not travel outside of the United States until you receive a [travel permit](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-advance-parole-travel-document-explained) (officially called an “[Advance Parole Travel Document](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-advance-parole-travel-document-explained)”). In addition, you’ll have to be present for interviews and appointments, either in the United States or at the U.S. embassy or consulate.

### Consular processing complications

When going through consular processing, there’s an added risk that your application could be flagged for “administrative review,” which means your application will undergo further screening. It’s unclear exactly why certain cases are flagged for review, but it generally means that an immigration officer has found some type of irregularity. This would add time — sometimes months — to the application process. During this period, it would also be difficult to find out the status of an application.

There is no danger of “administrative review,” however if you apply for a marriage-based green card from within the United States.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

### Frequently Asked Questions

When does the CR1 visa expire?

The CR-1 visa is valid for two years, after which the visa holder must apply to [remove the conditions](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/conditional-permanent-residency/) on the marriage green card to make it permanent.

What happens during a CR1 visa interview?

During the CR-1 interview, the immigration officer will ask questions to determine the authenticity of your marriage. You will be asked questions about your relationship, your daily life together, and any future plans. For more info, Boundless has put together a detailed guide on the [marriage green card interview](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/marriage-green-card-interview-explained/).

What is the income requirement for a CR-1 visa?

The U.S. sponsor must have an income of at least 125% of the Federal Poverty Guidelines for their household size and location. For couples with no children living in the 48 contiguous states, the current minimum annual income requirement is $25,550.

Who qualifies for adjustment of status?

To qualify for a marriage green card via adjustment of status, you must be living in the United States and married to a U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident.

How long does it take for adjustment of status?

The current wait time for adjustment of status after marriage is – months for the spouse of a U.S. citizen and – months for the spouse of a lawful permanent resident. .

For more info, Boundless has put together [an in-depth guide](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-long-does-marriage-green-card-take/) about adjustment of status wait times for a marriage green card.

Is adjustment of status the same as I-485?

Form I-485 is the immigration form used to apply for adjustment of status. You will fill out this form when applying for a green card from within the United States.

What is the average processing time for a CR1 visa?

The wait time for a CR-1 visa depends on if you’re married to a U.S. citizen or green card holder. The processing time for a U.S. citizen is currently between and months, while for a green card holder the wait time is and months.

Can CR1 visa holders work in the U.S.?

Yes, as a lawful permanent resident, CR1 visa holders are permitted to travel abroad. However, they should not remain outside the U.S. for more than one year as this may affect their eligibility to apply for U.S. citizenship or to reenter the country.
