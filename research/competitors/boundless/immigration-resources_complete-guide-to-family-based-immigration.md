## Understanding Family-Based Immigration Categories

The U.S. government divides family-based immigration into two main categories:

### Immediate Relatives of U.S. Citizens

Immediate relatives receive priority in the immigration system with no annual caps on visa numbers:

*   **Spouses of U.S. Citizens****(IR1/CR1)** – If you’re married to a U.S. citizen, you may qualify for a green card. [Learn more about the marriage-based green card process](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/marriage-based-green-cards-explained).
*   **Parents of U.S. Citizens****(IR5)** – U.S. citizens who are at least 21 years old can petition for their parents to become permanent residents. [Explore our parents green card guide](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/ir-5-parent-visa-explained).
*   **Children of U.S. Citizens****(IR2/CR2)** – Unmarried children under 21 of U.S. citizens can be sponsored for permanent residency. [Understand the process for children](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/ir-2-child-visa-explained).

### Family Preference Categories

These categories have annual numerical limitations and often involve longer wait times:

*   **First Preference (F1)**: Unmarried adult children (21 or older) of U.S. citizens.
*   **Second Preference (F2)**:
    *   F2A: Spouses and unmarried children (under 21) of permanent residents
    *   F2B: Unmarried adult children (21 or older) of permanent residents.

*   **Third Preference (F3)**: Married children of U.S. citizens, regardless of age.
*   **Fourth Preference (F4)**: Siblings of U.S. citizens, provided the U.S. citizen is at least 21 years old.

Learn more about annual caps on green cards with our guide on [how to read the Visa Bulletin](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-to-read-the-visa-bulletin).

## The Family-Based Immigration Process

1.   **File the Petition**
    *   The U.S. citizen or permanent resident family member (the petitioner) must file [Form I-130](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/form-i-130-explained) (“Petition for Alien Relative”), with USCIS.

2.   **Wait for USCIS Approval**
    *   After filing, USCIS will review your petition. [Processing times](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/uscis-processing-times) vary based on the family relationship and USCIS workload.

3.   **Visa Availability**
    *   Immediate relatives can proceed immediately after petition approval. Family preference categories must wait until a visa becomes available according to the [Visa Bulletin](https://www.boundless.com/blog/visa-bulletin/).

4.   **Apply for a Green Card**
    *   Depending on where the beneficiary lives, they will apply via one of the following processes:
        *   [**Consular Processing**](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/green-card-consular-processing): Apply for an immigrant visa at a U.S. embassy or consulate abroad.
        *   [**Adjustment of Status**](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/adjustment-status-process-explained): Apply to adjust status to permanent resident if already lawfully in the U.S.

5.   **Attend the Interview**
    *   All applicants must attend an interview. Learn more about [how to prepare for the green card interview.](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-green-card-interview-explained)

6.   **Receive Your Green Card**
    *   If approved, you’ll receive your [green card](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/the-green-card-explained) and begin your life as a permanent resident in the United States.

## Common Challenges in Family-Based Immigration

*   **Proving a Bona Fide Relationship**: Documents [demonstrating the authenticity](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-do-we-prove-our-marriage-is-real) of your family relationship are an important part of the application.
*   **Navigating Long Wait Times**: Family preference categories often face significant backlogs. [Understand how to check your place in line](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-to-read-the-visa-bulletin).
*   **Addressing Inadmissibility Issues**: Certain factors may make someone inadmissible to the U.S. [Explore how to apply for a waiver of inadmissibility](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/how-to-apply-waiver-of-inadmissibility).
*   **Understanding the Costs**: Immigration applications [involve various fees](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/uscis-gov-immigration-forms-and-fees)including filing fees, [medical exam](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/preparing-for-the-medical-exam) costs, and potential legal expenses.

## Special Considerations

*   **LGBTQ+ Families**: Same-sex spouses [have the same rights](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/same-sex-relationships-and-marriage-based-green-cards) in the immigration process as opposite-sex couples.
*   **Adopted Children**: Learn about the process for [international adoptions](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/derivative-citizenship-explained).
*   **Conditional Residency**: Spouses married less than two years receive [conditional status](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/cr1-ir1-spouse-visa) initially.
*   **Financial Requirements**: All sponsors must meet [income requirements](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/income-requirements-for-green-card).
